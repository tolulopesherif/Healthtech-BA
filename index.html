Power BI · DAX

Business Context

VitaCore needed a single dashboard that could show leadership where revenue and profit were actually coming from — by product, by sales rep, by region and channel — instead of pulling separate reports for each question.

What I Built

MediCore Health Distribution

Branded Distribution Analytics Dashboard
Power BI · Star Schema

Business Context

MediCore needed a dashboard that didn't just report numbers but was built on a data model that could scale — proper fact and dimension tables rather than one flat sheet with everything crammed in.

What I Built

A star schema data model underneath the dashboard, with a colour palette extracted directly from MediCore's own logo so the reporting matched their brand rather than using a generic Power BI theme. Getting the schema right meant troubleshooting dimension table relationships and fixing the Σ (summarize) symbol issue that Power BI throws when a dimension field gets treated as measurable data instead of a category. The finished report runs three interactive pages — Executive Summary, Sales Analysis, and Recommendation — using line, donut, clustered bar, column, bar, ribbon, and funnel charts, with slicers on Month, Sales_Region, Channel, and Year, pulling from a Fact_Sales table joined against Location, Product, Customer, Distributor, Sales, and Month dimension tables.

Fact/dimension structure, coloured with MediCore's actual brand palette pulled from the .pbix theme file

Page 3 — Recommendation

A dedicated page turning the Sales Analysis findings into nine concrete action items for MediCore's commercial team:

1. Prioritise high-revenue regions
Double down on regions already proving product-market fit — increase inventory allocation to avoid stockouts during peak periods, and replicate winning pricing and distributor tactics in mid-tier markets.
2. Turn around low-revenue countries
Diagnose each underperforming country individually against four possible causes — demand, pricing, availability, distribution — using sales funnels and stockout frequency rather than treating "underperforming" as one problem.
3. Strengthen seasonal sales planning
Use the monthly trend data as a forecasting engine — increase stock and marketing spend 4–6 weeks ahead of peak months, and use tactical discounts to smooth out the slow periods.
4. Lift average order value
Bundle complementary healthcare products and offer volume discounts on repeat-use items — revenue growth without raising acquisition cost.
5. Improve customer retention
Loyalty programmes tied to purchase frequency, personalised offers based on past purchases, and automated reactivation campaigns for customers inactive 60–90 days.
6. Optimise pricing for profitability
Identify high-volume, thin-margin SKUs and test small price increases (2–5%) where demand elasticity is low, benchmarked against competitor pricing.
7. Expand best-selling products
Increase inventory depth on top sellers, promote them harder in ads and email, and introduce complementary SKUs to drive cross-sell.
8. Monitor sales KPIs through dashboards
Track Total Revenue, Revenue Growth %, Total Orders, AOV, Customer Growth, and Revenue by Region/Country as a daily command centre, with drill-through views for product- and region-level diagnostics.
9. Forecast future demand
Build a rolling 3–6 month forecast off historical monthly trends and share it with procurement and logistics to cut delays.

TrueNorth Health Network

Patient Appointment No-Show Prediction Analysis
Excel · PivotTables

Business Context

TrueNorth wanted to understand where and why patients were missing appointments, across seven countries and multiple branches and specialties, so they could target scheduling changes rather than applying a blanket fix. The brief broke down into five specific requirements: no-show rate by country/branch, no-show rate by specialty, demographic patterns by age and gender, whether prior no-show history predicts future no-shows, and whether scheduling gap (wait days) affects no-show likelihood.

Key Findings

No-shows by country and branch
UK led at 264, followed by Netherlands (261), France (250), Spain (245), Italy (242), Canada (239), and Germany (235) — a fairly even spread with no major outlier. By branch, WestCare Outpatient held the largest share at 21.0% of all no-shows, with Eastgate, Northside, Central Clinic, and Riverside close behind.
No-shows by specialty
General Practice accounted for 506 no-shows, more than double the next closest specialty, Diagnostics (407). Mental Health, Pediatrics, Cardiology, Orthopedics, and Dermatology followed in descending order.
Age and gender patterns
The 45-54 age band had the highest no-show count (344), closely followed by 35-44 (340). The 15-24 band was lowest at 217. Gender was roughly even across all bands.
Prior no-show history
61.6% of no-shows came from patients with zero prior no-shows, 20.9% from patients with one prior no-show, dropping to single digits from there. This is share of total no-shows per bucket, not a normalized rate — so on its own it doesn't prove repeat no-show patients are higher risk; that would need the rate calculated per bucket against total appointments booked, which wasn't available in this dataset. Sum of previous no-shows across the dataset came to 1,589.
Wait time and no-shows
No-shows concentrated in the 13-22 day wait window (33.8%) and the 3-12 day window (25.6%). One bucket showed negative wait days at 14.9% of no-shows — a data quality issue in the source data (Wait_Days going negative), not an actual scheduling pattern.
No-show counts by specialty — General Practice more than double the next highest

Recommendations

The workbook's Recommendation sheet turns the findings into a phased digital transformation strategy rather than a list of quick fixes:

1. Implement an Integrated Health Information System
A unified digital platform consolidating patient records, care coordination, and operational reporting, to reduce fragmentation and support faster decision-making across departments.
2. Strengthen Data Governance & Reporting
Structured data governance policies for consistency and reliability across reporting outputs, with automated dashboards giving leadership real-time visibility into performance metrics.
3. Enhance Patient Experience Through Digital Touchpoints
Online scheduling, secure messaging, and personalised care reminders to improve satisfaction and cut administrative load.
4. Build Scalable Infrastructure for Future Growth
Cloud-ready, modular architecture so TrueNorth can expand services and integrate new technologies without costly system overhauls.
5. Prioritise Staff Training & Change Management
Structured training and clear communication to accelerate adoption and minimise disruption during the transition.

HealthBridge Clinics

Improving Patient Experience Through Waiting Time & Satisfaction Analysis
Excel · PivotCharts

Business Context

HealthBridge operates outpatient clinics across multiple locations offering general medicine, emergency care, pediatrics, maternity, and dental services. Management flagged long waiting times at certain branches and departments and wanted to know whether that was actually hurting satisfaction, and where to act first.

Key Findings

Highest patient volume
General Medicine received the most visits by far — 327, or 32.7% of all patients — ahead of Pediatrics (165), Emergency (160), Maternity (131), Dental (111), and Laboratory (106). By branch, Central Clinic led with 296 visits, ahead of Northside (203), Riverside (197), Hillview (167), and Eastgate (137).
Longest average wait
Emergency had the longest average wait at 62.39 minutes, well ahead of Maternity (43.95), General Medicine (39.73), Pediatrics (31.90), Dental (27.31), and Laboratory (23.67) — notably, Emergency didn't have the highest visit volume, so the wait isn't purely a volume problem. Laboratory posted the best wait time in the network at 23.67 minutes.
Lowest satisfaction
Central Clinic scored lowest on satisfaction at 3.50, with Hillview close behind at 3.51 — the same branch with the highest visit volume (Central) also has the lowest satisfaction score. Riverside scored highest at 3.93.
Does wait time actually reduce satisfaction?
Yes, and the effect is large: satisfaction runs at 4.68 in the Low wait band (0-15 min) and 4.37 in Moderate (16-30 min), then drops to 3.45 in High (31-60 min) and falls sharply to 2.41 in Very High (61+ min) — a drop of over 2 full points from best to worst band. One build note: the PivotChart's x-axis was sorted alphabetically (High, Low, Moderate, Very High) rather than by actual wait-time order — the trend still holds once read correctly, but the chart itself needed the categories reordered to present cleanly.
Satisfaction falls sharply once wait time crosses into the Very High band — corrected to actual time order

Recommendations

Written directly off the pivot output, tied back to the specific number each one addresses:

1. Staff General Medicine for volume
General Medicine handles 327 patients — 32.7% of the total — and needs staffing levels that reflect that share rather than an even split across departments.
2. Fix Emergency wait times first
At 62.39 minutes, Emergency's average wait is the network's most urgent problem — triage fast-tracking and additional staff needed immediately, independent of any broader rollout.
3. Redistribute Central Clinic's load
Central Clinic is overloaded at 296 visits against Eastgate's 137 and Hillview's 167 — rebalancing patient flow toward the lower-volume branches would ease the pressure driving Central's satisfaction score down.
4. Investigate Central Clinic directly
Central's 3.50 satisfaction score is the lowest in the network and sits alongside its highest visit volume — worth investigating facilities and staffing specifically at that branch rather than assuming volume alone explains it.
5. Treat wait time as the top lever on satisfaction
The drop from 4.68 to 2.41 across wait bands confirms a strong relationship — cutting wait times is the single highest-leverage action available for improving satisfaction network-wide.
6. Study and replicate Laboratory's process
Laboratory posts the best average wait time in the network at 23.67 minutes — understanding what it does differently and applying that across other departments is a lower-cost lever than adding headcount everywhere.

Asian Public Health Dataset

Immunization Programme Analysis
PostgreSQL · SQL

Business Context

Governments across Asia are investing heavily in immunization programmes to prevent vaccine-preventable disease. The Ministry of Health needed the database queried to understand which vaccines were being administered most, which countries had the highest coverage, and where trends were moving — to support resourcing decisions with evidence rather than assumption.

What I Built

A set of PostgreSQL queries covering joins across vaccines, countries, and regions, aggregation by dose counts and per-capita rates, and year-over-year trend comparisons.

34.36M
Total Doses Administered
41.1%
Cumulative Growth
Total doses came from a full-table SUM (34,362,000). Cumulative growth of 41.1% was calculated as (13,365,000 − 9,469,000) ÷ 9,469,000. The more interesting finding was in the contrast between two queries: raw country totals and per-capita rates told opposite stories — Uzbekistan topped the per-capita ranking but was nowhere near the top on raw totals, which shows population size was doing most of the work in the raw numbers and would have been missed without running both queries side by side.
